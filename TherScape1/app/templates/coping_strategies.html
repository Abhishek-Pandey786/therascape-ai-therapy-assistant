Cognitive Reframing

Cognitive reframing is a technique used to shift your perspective and identify alternative ways of viewing situations, behaviors, and thoughts.

How to Practice Cognitive Reframing

  1. Identify the negative thought. Be specific about what you're thinking and feeling.
  2. Challenge the thought. Ask yourself: Is this thought based on facts or feelings? Is there evidence that contradicts this thought?
  3. Find alternative perspectives. What are other ways to view this situation? What would you tell a friend in the same situation?
  4. Create a balanced thought. Develop a new thought that incorporates a more balanced view of the situation.
  5. Practice regularly. Like any skill, cognitive reframing takes practice. Keep working at it, even if it feels difficult at first.

Example:

cancel Negative Thought

"I made a mistake on that presentation. I'm terrible at my job and everyone thinks I'm incompetent."

check_circle Reframed Thought

"I made a mistake during the presentation, which is something that happens to everyone. This one error doesn't define my overall performance. I can learn from this experience and improve for next time."

Grounding Techniques for Anxiety

Grounding techniques help you stay in the present moment when you're feeling overwhelmed, anxious, or experiencing distressing thoughts.

The 5-4-3-2-1 Exercise

This technique engages all five senses to help you focus on the present:

  • 5: Identify FIVE things you can see around you
  • 4: Identify FOUR things you can touch or feel
  • 3: Identify THREE things you can hear
  • 2: Identify TWO things you can smell
  • 1: Identify ONE thing you can taste

Take your time with each step, focusing your attention on each sensation.

Physical Grounding

  • Hold a piece of ice in your hand
  • Splash cold water on your face
  • Dig your feet firmly into the ground
  • Focus on your breathing - slow and deep
  • Name objects around you categorized by color

Emotional Regulation Strategies

These techniques help you manage intense emotions when they arise, allowing you to respond rather than react.

STOP Technique

  • S - Stop what you're doing
  • T - Take a step back
  • O - Observe what's happening internally and externally
  • P - Proceed mindfully with awareness

Self-Soothing With the Five Senses

Create a toolkit of soothing activities for each sense:

  • Vision: Look at beautiful or calming images, watch the sunset, or observe nature
  • Hearing: Listen to soothing music, nature sounds, or guided meditations
  • Smell: Use essential oils, light a scented candle, or smell fresh flowers
  • Taste: Savor a favorite food or drink, focus on the flavors and textures
  • Touch: Take a warm bath, wrap in a soft blanket, or hold a stress ball
